Process

I explain in depth how my nighttime gallows-frame photograph was captured, composed, and edited.

This image of a gallows frame is one of what I consider to be my best works. I was driving through uptown Butte at night when I noticed the structure lit up. It was a dry, calm night—not too chilly, as it was the early start of fall. I had just moved into my off-campus apartment. After meeting several of my neighbors, I asked if I could climb to the top of their fire escape for a photo opportunity. Right in front of me was an old mining device, a gallows frame lit up with red LED lights spanning its entire shape.

I knelt down on one knee—you see, I like to get a sense of how the object feels relative to the viewer. I enjoy conveying the feeling of being overtaken or overwhelmed by the subject, so a lower angle for a more towering effect was my approach. I used an OSMO 6 phone mount, which has become a recent necessity for my content creation in general, but it has been amazing for my still images.

Most of the magic for this image came from the editing. I’ve used Photoshop for a while, and I took a class on it in high school back in 2019. The original image I took was a bit fuzzy and had some color bleed and noise. For this image in particular, I utilized the Camera Raw Filter section to grade the color and restore sharp contrast. I cleaned the blacks, restored the red LEDs, adjusted noise reduction in small increments, and refined the sharpening.
